# Digital Life Coaching Service Personas

---

**Persona 1: Marcus Webb, 33, Male, Full-Stack Developer, United Kingdom**

Marcus is a full-time software developer in his early thirties who spends between nine and twelve hours online daily, primarily for work, and finishes most days feeling drained and dissatisfied with how his non-work hours disappear into passive scrolling.

His end goals when using a digital life coaching service are to establish a structured routine that separates purposeful online activity from passive consumption so he can reclaim at least two hours each evening for intentional offline pursuits; to develop a reliable personal system for recognising when he is scrolling out of habit versus genuine need and interrupting that pattern before it takes hold; and to use digital tools more deliberately for learning and skill development rather than entertainment, so that his screen time feels earned and productive.

Beyond the product itself, Marcus wants to feel like an autonomous, self-determined person who is not controlled by the platforms he uses. He aspires to build a life in which technology serves his values rather than subverts them, and where his focus and mental energy are available for the relationships and creative projects that matter most to him.

During his interactions with the coaching service, Marcus wants to feel competent and in control, not lectured or shamed. He wants the experience to feel like working with a sharp, evidence-based thinking partner rather than receiving generic wellness advice.

His observable behavioural patterns reflect these goals directly. He defaults to Google searching when he encounters dubious information online, reflecting a habit of seeking verification rather than accepting things passively, yet he still falls into social media and video loops on evenings and weekends, revealing that awareness alone is insufficient without structure. He reports often thinking about the implications of AI on his daily life and is moderately worried about job disruption, which means coaching conversations touching on intentional AI use will resonate with him. When digitally overwhelmed, he takes a break rather than talks to someone, suggesting he prefers solitary coping strategies, which the coaching experience should accommodate by offering solo reflection tools alongside any community or accountability features. He trusts academics and experts above other sources, meaning credibility signals such as cited research and evidence-based frameworks will be important to his engagement with the service.

Marcus opens the coaching app on a Thursday evening after realising he has spent ninety minutes scrolling through news and social media without any clear intention. He feels the familiar combination of fatigue and mild self-reproach he described as "drained" in the survey. He launches a session and is prompted to log what he actually wanted to do after work versus what he ended up doing. The gap is obvious and uncomfortable. His coach surfaces a short framework for designing digital boundaries around his specific workday pattern and suggests a two-week experiment with a hard cutoff for recreational scrolling at eight in the evening. Marcus sets the experiment, schedules a check-in, and closes the app feeling that he has done something concrete rather than simply felt guilty again.

**Persona 2: Kezia Adeyemi, 28, Female, Marketing Manager, United States**

Kezia is a full-time marketing manager in her late twenties who spends seven to nine hours online daily, much of it professionally necessary, and who struggles with an almost-always present feeling of self-consciousness and inadequacy that intensifies after time on social media platforms central to her job.

Her end goals with a digital life coaching service are to reduce the amount of time she spends doom scrolling social media during her personal hours, particularly in the evenings when it feeds comparison and anxious thinking; to build enough psychological distance from social media content to do her job without internalising its emotional costs; and to identify and commit to at least two offline activities that reliably restore her energy after high-screen workdays, so she stops defaulting to more screen time as a recovery strategy.

At the life level, Kezia wants to feel genuinely good about herself independently of what she sees online. She grew up alongside social media and has a deep, half-conscious sense that her self-worth has become entangled with digital comparison, and she wants to untangle it. She aspires to have a stable internal sense of identity that does not contract when she sees filtered images or curated life highlights.

During her interactions with the coaching service, Kezia wants to feel seen without being pathologised. She wants the service to acknowledge that her problem is structural and widespread, not a personal failing, and she wants practical recommendations that respect the fact that she cannot simply quit the platforms she is paid to manage.

Her key behavioural patterns flow directly from these goals. She reports feeling lonely or disconnected after spending time online on a frequent basis, and she describes feeling inadequate or unworthy after social media sessions, linking her end goal of building self-distance to a lived, recurring experience the service can address specifically. She often regrets the amount of time she spends online and when overwhelmed says she takes a break, but her survey responses suggest these breaks are not restorative enough to change the pattern, indicating she needs coaching on what a break should actually contain. She trusts academics and experts and looks for multiple sources when evaluating content, reflecting an educated, evidence-seeking orientation that means she will engage seriously with research-backed coaching content. She is very bothered by AI-generated content because it undermines her trust in the visual and social information she processes constantly, making her acutely interested in media literacy framing.

It is a Sunday afternoon and Kezia has just spent two hours on Instagram that began as competitive research for a client campaign and blurred into personal comparison browsing. She feels simultaneously informed about industry trends and hollow about her own life. She opens the coaching service and lands on a check-in prompt asking her to name the emotional state she arrived at the app with. She types "inadequate" and the service reflects that word back to her, drawing on data she has logged previously to note this is the third Sunday in a row. A short coaching thread helps her distinguish between professional use and personal use on the same apps, and she drafts a simple rule for herself. She books a Wednesday session to follow up.

**Persona 3: Sandra Holloway, 52, Female, Part-Time Administrator, United Kingdom**

Sandra is a part-time administrator in her early fifties who spends five to six hours online daily and reports feeling neutral-to-tired afterwards, but whose deeper struggle is with the steady erosion of her trust in the information environment and a growing sense that she cannot tell what is real, what is AI-generated, and who or what is trustworthy online.

Her end goals with a digital life coaching service are to develop a reliable, simple personal process for evaluating whether online content is trustworthy before she shares or acts on it, so she no longer feels naive or easily misled; to spend less time on social media and news platforms that leave her feeling bored and misinformed, replacing those hours with activities more aligned with her values such as family connection and offline learning; and to feel confident enough about her digital habits that she stops worrying that she is being manipulated without knowing it.

Sandra's broader life aspiration is to feel like an informed, dignified adult in a digital world that often makes her feel foolish and behind. She wants to maintain meaningful relationships with her family and wider community and believes excessive and poorly directed online activity is quietly undermining her presence in those relationships.

When interacting with a coaching service, Sandra wants to feel respected and not condescended to. She is aware that her digital literacy is not as high as younger people's and is sensitive to anything that implies she should already know how to handle these challenges. She wants guidance that feels practical, warm, and grounded.

Her behavioural patterns connect clearly to her goals. She reports questioning whether online content is real or trustworthy often or almost always, and feeling annoyed and duped when she discovers she has been misled, making media literacy and content evaluation a high-priority coaching topic for her. She trusts traditional news and family and friends more than she trusts independent creators or algorithms, reflecting a preference for established, human sources that the service should acknowledge and work with rather than against. She frequently regrets the time spent on social media and says she would have preferred to do something meaningful or relaxing instead, directly linking her end goal of reducing low-value digital time. When overwhelmed she takes a break, but she also reports wanting to talk to someone, suggesting she would engage with a coaching format that includes a conversational, human-feeling element rather than purely automated tools. She is only slightly confident she can distinguish human from AI content and is quite worried about AI undermining human creativity, which grounds her in a specific, actionable anxiety the service can address.

Sandra is reading a news article on Facebook shared by a neighbour that claims a local health service has been privatised. Something feels off but she cannot articulate why. She remembers the coaching service and opens it, navigating to a trust-evaluation module she bookmarked last week. She works through three quick questions about the source, the headline, and whether she can find corroboration from a known institution. Within four minutes she has concluded the article is misleading and found the accurate context from a local council website. She feels competent rather than duped. She makes a note to bring this up in her next scheduled coaching check-in and considers recommending the service to her sister.

**Persona 4: Tyler Goss, 22, Male, Part-Time Retail Worker, Canada**

Tyler is a twenty-two-year-old part-time retail worker who spends five to nine hours online daily, mostly on social media, gaming, and short-form video, and who reports feeling energised in the moment but consistently guilty, drained, and embarrassed afterwards, with a growing awareness that his digital habits are blocking the personal goals he genuinely cares about.

His end goals with a digital life coaching service are to reduce the total time he spends on entertainment and passive consumption platforms by replacing that time with purposeful activities aligned with learning, fitness, or future career development; to understand and interrupt the psychological mechanisms that pull him back to his phone when he consciously intended to stop, so that his intentions and his behaviour start matching more often; and to build enough positive momentum from small offline achievements that his sense of self-worth stops depending so heavily on online validation.

Tyler's life goal is to feel like he is building something real and moving forward. He is acutely aware that his peers are forming careers and relationships and he sometimes feels stuck, with digital consumption filling the space where purposeful action could be. He wants to feel proud of how he spends his time, not relieved when the day is over.

When interacting with the coaching service, Tyler wants to feel energised and like he is being trusted as an intelligent adult, not treated as someone with a problem to be fixed. He is drawn to progress-based framing and would respond well to streaks, clear milestones, and coaching that feels like a challenge rather than a lecture.

His behaviours map to his goals precisely. He often regrets the amount of time he spends online and identifies social media and videos as his least-trusted and most-regretted content, yet returns to both, revealing the gap between stated values and actual behaviour that coaching is positioned to bridge. He is quite worried about AI disrupting his life, including his creative and professional future, indicating he is motivated by forward-looking concerns that can be channelled into action rather than anxiety. He looks for multiple sources when evaluating content and trusts academics and experts, suggesting a latent capacity for critical engagement that is currently underutilised. He reports being moderately confident in his ability to detect AI content but is bothered by it to a significant degree, which means concerns about authenticity online can be framed as a lever for developing more intentional media habits. When digitally overwhelmed he exercises, which the service should recognise and build on as an existing coping strength.

Tyler has just finished a four-hour session of short-form video watching on a Saturday afternoon and feels that specific combination of wired and guilty he knows well. He opens the coaching app, which he downloaded three weeks ago but has only used twice. A check-in prompt asks him what he was supposed to do today. He types "look into night courses." A coaching thread connects his stated value of improving himself for the future with the specific time he just spent, then walks him through a ten-minute exercise in which he books a single first step toward the course search. The thread ends with a challenge to open the course website before he picks up his phone for entertainment again. Tyler completes it that same evening.
