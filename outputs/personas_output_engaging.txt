# Digital Life Coaching Personas

Based on the 1,003-respondent dataset, the data reveals five meaningfully distinct clusters: burned-out professionals drowning in screen time, values-driven but digitally conflicted working parents, anxious young adults caught in social comparison loops, older digitally cautious knowledge-seekers, and high-usage individuals experiencing genuine digital dependency. Five personas adequately represent these patterns without over-segmenting.

---

## Persona 1: Marcus Oyelaran

**Photo description:** A Black British man in his early thirties, slightly tired eyes behind thin-rimmed glasses, wearing a crumpled open-collar shirt. He is sitting at a cluttered home office desk with two monitors glowing behind him. His expression is thoughtful but strained, the kind of face that has been reading screens for too long.

Marcus is thirty-one years old, the son of Nigerian parents who moved to East London before he was born. He grew up watching his father work double shifts and inherited an acute sense that self-improvement was both a duty and a survival strategy. He studied computer science at a post-1992 university, the first in his family to complete a degree, and now works full-time in software development in the UK. He lives alone in a one-bedroom flat in Hackney, paying rent that feels perpetually too high, and visits his mother in Tottenham most Sundays.

On a typical weekday, Marcus is online before he has finished his first coffee, checking Slack messages that accumulated overnight. He spends roughly nine to ten hours online for work, then finds himself sliding into another hour or two of passive scrolling, Reddit threads about tech ethics, YouTube breakdowns of news events, the odd football highlight. By 10 p.m. he feels what he describes in his survey as "bleak" — not quite depressed, but hollow. He gets his best thinking done between six and eight in the morning, before the notifications begin, and those quiet hours are the ones he protects most fiercely. He occasionally goes for a run along the canal after work, and that hour feels like the only true break he gets.

The most important relationship in Marcus's digital life is his group chat with three university friends scattered across the country; it is the only online space where he laughs without feeling manipulated. His line manager expects instant Slack responses, a norm Marcus resents but complies with because remote working feels precarious. He has a loose network of LinkedIn connections he scrolls past with a mixture of envy and scepticism. His closest friend Dele, who deleted most social media two years ago, keeps telling Marcus he is "cooked" by the internet, and Marcus suspects Dele is right.

Marcus aspires to eventually build something of his own, a side project that reflects his actual values rather than whoever is paying him. He also wants to read more physical books and reclaim the long, absorbing focus he remembers from his twenties. He is anxious about AI displacing mid-level developers, which hits uncomfortably close to his daily work, and he worries that constant algorithmic noise is genuinely eroding his ability to concentrate for more than twenty minutes. He is also anxious that his heavy screen time is slowly hollowing out his social life in ways he does not yet fully see.

Marcus is simultaneously a natural target for a life coaching product and someone who will approach it with healthy suspicion. He has tried productivity apps before, Notion, Todoist, a time-blocking spreadsheet he abandoned in week three, and he is wary of anything that feels like "another thing to manage." What would genuinely interest him is a service that helps him identify which of his online hours are actually serving his goals and which are simply filling an absence. He would respond to evidence-based framing, would appreciate a tool that did not feel preachy, and would likely become a vocal advocate if it delivered even modest, concrete results. He worries slightly that a coaching product might just be "another algorithm pretending to care."

## Persona 2: Siobhan Gallagher-Webb

**Photo description:** A white Irish-British woman in her late thirties, warm but slightly harried expression, dark hair pulled back in a practical bun. She is photographed in a bright kitchen, a child's drawing visible on the fridge behind her and a half-drunk cup of tea on the counter. She is dressed for work but her cardigan has been draped over the chair.

Siobhan is thirty-nine, originally from County Kerry, and moved to Birmingham in her mid-twenties following a post-graduate nursing qualification. She now works part-time as a community health nurse and spends the rest of her time as the primary carer for two children, aged seven and four, with her partner Dan who works long hours in construction management. She grew up in a household where her mother ran the local post office and her father was involved in Gaelic football; collective responsibility and showing up for people are deeply baked into who she is. She attended mass until her late twenties and, while no longer practising, still organises herself around a firm ethical code she finds it hard to articulate but easy to feel.

Siobhan's mornings are a relay race: school runs, Dan out the door, a brief window of clinical notes before the younger child needs something. Her online life happens in fragments — ten minutes on her phone between home visits, a longer stretch after the children are in bed when she half-watches something on Netflix while simultaneously scrolling Instagram. She follows a handful of nursing advocacy accounts and several parenting pages, and feels obscurely bad about herself most nights after the Instagram session. She describes her post-social-media feeling as "ugly" in one survey response and in another as "disconnected." She turns to Google for health queries before anyone else, and trusts academic and professional sources strongly.

Siobhan's social support is genuinely warm but often digital by necessity: a WhatsApp group with her nursing cohort, a smaller one with secondary school friends back in Kerry, and a neighbour named Priya with whom she exchanges voice notes rather than texts because both of them are too busy to type. Dan is supportive but travels a lot, and Siobhan is lonely in the particular way of someone surrounded by people and obligations but with very little space that is purely her own. She occasionally feels envious of the curated lives she sees on Instagram and then feels embarrassed by the envy.

She wants, more than anything, to feel less reactive and more intentional, both with her time online and with her life more broadly. She has a persistent, quiet aspiration to train as a health visitor or eventually move into a palliative care advisory role, but she cannot seem to find the mental space to make the application. She is anxious about her children's future relationship with screens and whether she is modelling the right behaviour; she is also anxious about misinformation in health contexts, which she encounters regularly in her clinical work and finds frightening. She worries about AI undermining her professional expertise, particularly as patients are increasingly arriving at consultations with AI-generated health summaries they treat as gospel.

Siobhan would be drawn to a digital coaching product if it spoke to her in the language of energy management and values-alignment rather than optimisation or productivity. She would appreciate something that acknowledged the genuine structural constraints of her life, including the caregiving responsibilities that make a simple "just spend less time online" recommendation feel insulting. She would trust the product more if it cited research, had a human element, and felt distinct from the social media ecosystem she is trying to partially disengage from. Her biggest hesitation would be time: she would need to believe that the product itself did not become another obligation.

## Persona 3: Jaylen Carter

**Photo description:** A young Black American man of twenty-three, bright-eyed but with the slightly dazed look of someone who has been up too late. He is wearing a hoodie with a small university logo, earbuds around his neck, photographed in what appears to be a coffee shop or library study room. His phone is face-down on the table next to a half-eaten granola bar.

Jaylen is twenty-three and grew up in a mid-sized city in the American South, the middle child of a schoolteacher mother and a father who drove long-haul trucks for most of Jaylen's childhood. He is currently enrolled in a graduate programme in public health and works part-time to cover his living expenses, which means most weeks he is running on insufficient sleep and too much caffeine. He is thoughtful, politically engaged, and quick to notice injustice, which makes his social media usage both genuinely informative and genuinely painful. He has been online in a serious way since he was about twelve and regards the internet with the ambivalent intimacy of someone who grew up inside it.

On a typical day, Jaylen wakes to his phone. He checks Twitter (which he still calls Twitter), his course email, and three different group chats before he has left his bed. By eleven in the morning he has usually been online for four hours and feels, as he put it in his survey, "drained" and "sad" despite not having done anything he would call harmful. He spends eleven to twelve hours online on most days, a number that alarmed him when he actually calculated it. He takes short breaks by going to the gym three times a week, which he describes as the one hour of the day he does not feel like he is performing himself for an audience. He studies at a coffee shop called Grounded on Tuesday and Thursday afternoons because leaving his apartment is the only reliable way he can actually focus.

Jaylen's most important digital relationship is a Discord server with six friends from his undergraduate years; they share memes, check in on each other's mental health in surprisingly direct ways, and have what Jaylen considers genuine community. He is also in an ongoing, faintly exhausting situationship with someone he met on Hinge who communicates primarily through memes and occasional disappearances, which has made Jaylen more attuned than he would like to be to response times and read receipts. His cohort in the public health programme is supportive but competitive, and LinkedIn makes that competition visible in ways that sometimes spiral him into comparison anxiety.

Jaylen aspires to work in health equity research, specifically on how structural conditions shape digital health access in underserved communities — an aspiration he finds meaningful and, some days, impossibly distant. He wants to be someone who reads long-form journalism, finishes the books he starts, and does not spend forty-five minutes rage-scrolling before bed. He is anxious about AI in the context of academic integrity — he has watched classmates use it in ways he considers dishonest, and he is uncertain where he stands — and he is also anxious about AI generating content designed to look like Black creators' work without their consent. He worries that social media is making him performatively outraged rather than genuinely engaged with the causes he cares about.

Jaylen's attitude toward a digital coaching product is shaped by two things: a genuine desire to change and a high radar for inauthenticity. He has tried deleting apps, setting screen time limits (which he bypasses), and one-week digital detoxes that collapse after three days. He would be interested in something that helped him understand the emotional triggers behind his scrolling rather than simply limiting his access to it. He would trust the product if it acknowledged structural context, did not assume he had the time or money for elaborate routines, and had some genuine diversity in its design team. He would be put off by anything that felt like wellness content dressed up as coaching, or anything that looked like it was built for a default user who was not him.

## Persona 4: Carol Whitfield

**Photo description:** A white American woman of fifty-three, silver-streaked hair worn down to her shoulders, reading glasses pushed up on her forehead. She is photographed at a kitchen table with a physical newspaper and a laptop both open in front of her, a large mug of coffee nearby. Her expression is alert, slightly sceptical, the face of someone who reads everything twice.

Carol is fifty-three and lives in a mid-sized city in the American Midwest. She grew up in a Methodist household in rural Ohio, the daughter of a librarian and a high school history teacher, and absorbed from both parents a deep belief that knowing things accurately and thinking critically are moral as well as intellectual obligations. She studied journalism as an undergraduate, worked at a regional newspaper for twelve years, and then moved into communications consulting when the paper restructured. She has been divorced for eight years and has two adult children who live in different states; she is close to both of them but sees them only a handful of times a year, which she manages with FaceTime calls that she simultaneously finds meaningful and slightly melancholy.

Carol's day begins with the newspaper — an actual physical one she has maintained a subscription to through two rounds of guilt about the cost — and a BBC podcast while she makes coffee. She is online for nine to ten hours on workdays, much of it for professional purposes, but she is honest with herself that a significant portion is news consumption that shades into doomscrolling by late afternoon. She describes her post-online-session feeling as "sluggish" and frequently picks up her phone to check on a news story and finds herself still scrolling twenty minutes later. She goes to a Tuesday evening yoga class at a studio called Root & Rise three blocks from her apartment, which she values enormously and protects with unusual firmness. She reads fiction before bed when she manages it.

Carol's most important digital relationship is with her children; she maintains long email threads with each of them, which feel more considered to her than text messages. She also has a small, tight group of former colleagues with whom she exchanges articles and long WhatsApp messages, a practice that distinguishes itself in her mind from passive social media consumption because it involves genuine thought. She is frustrated by Facebook, which she uses only to stay in touch with relatives and keep up with local community news, and which she finds increasingly cluttered with AI-generated content she cannot reliably identify. She trusts academics and established institutions for information and is deeply suspicious of social media and image-based content.

Carol wants to feel more in control of her information diet, to go back to the quality of attention she had in her forties when she could read a long piece all the way through without her mind drifting. She also wants to find a way to engage with current events without feeling depleted by them every evening; she considers being informed a responsibility, but knows she has blurred the line between being informed and being overwhelmed. She is anxious about AI in ways she can articulate precisely: she is concerned about synthetic media undermining journalism, about AI-generated health misinformation reaching her elderly mother, and about what happens to public epistemology when nobody can agree on what is real. She worries less about AI disrupting her own career than about what it is doing to collective trust.

Carol would approach a digital coaching service with both genuine interest and professional scepticism. She would read the about page, look for named researchers or advisors, and decide within the first ten minutes whether the product was serious or not. She would respond well to a service that treated her as a capable adult navigating a genuinely difficult information environment rather than a passive victim of her own habits. She would be interested in evidence-based tools for building more intentional news and information consumption patterns, and in support around re-establishing the long-focus reading habits she has partly lost. Her hesitation would be about privacy — she would want to understand clearly what data the service used and how — and about whether the service could offer anything she had not already tried through her own considerable discipline.
