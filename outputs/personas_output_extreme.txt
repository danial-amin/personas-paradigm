**Persona One**

Wiremu Tane is 34 years old, a full-time remote software contractor based in Auckland, New Zealand, originally from a small rural Māori community in the Waikato region. He relocated for work a decade ago and now earns his living almost entirely through digital platforms, spending more than twelve hours online daily. His situation places him at the outer edge of the dataset's usage distribution: while the modal respondent reports five to six hours online per day, Wiremu's professional dependency means he cannot meaningfully reduce his screen time without direct financial consequence, a constraint the survey's design does not accommodate. His primary daily activity is classified as work, yet the boundary between productive labour and passive consumption has entirely dissolved for him — a condition the questionnaire's binary framing between "work" and "unemployed" fails to capture.

The defining extreme characteristics shaping Wiremu's experience are his involuntary high-volume usage and his geographic and cultural peripherality within a dataset that skews heavily toward the United States and United Kingdom. He is among the roughly eight percent of respondents reporting more than twelve hours online, but unlike many in that cohort who report feeling energised or neutral, Wiremu reports exhaustion as his dominant post-session state — consistent with the dataset's pattern showing exhaustion clustering in the twelve-plus-hours group. More critically, he occupies a non-Western, Indigenous Pacific identity in a survey population that is seventy percent White and heavily Anglophone. The frameworks through which the survey assesses wellbeing, trust, and values are culturally tethered: questions about "connecting with family" assume proximity and bandwidth that rural communities may not have, and questions about "personal values" are anchored to individualistic, productivity-oriented conceptions of meaning that may sit uneasily alongside relational or communal Indigenous frameworks.

Wiremu's specific needs that the current design likely fails to meet include: a way to distinguish between hours online that are structurally coerced by employment versus freely chosen, since the self-estimate question conflates the two entirely; culturally contextualised language around trust and community that does not default to Western institutional anchors such as "government" or "academics" as the primary trust sources; recognition that his coping strategy of "taking a break" is economically inaccessible during working hours, making the dominant coping taxonomy in the survey inapplicable; and the ability to express that AI's disruption of his work is simultaneously his greatest professional concern and his most immediate daily tool, a contradiction the binary worry-scale cannot hold.

The barriers Wiremu faces are concrete. When asked how he copes when overwhelmed by digital life, his honest answer — "I don't, because stopping costs money" — has no representable option in a list that includes "exercise," "take a break," and "turn off phone." The survey assumes a leisure relationship with digital life even for those who have none. Additionally, the trust-source question presents "academics and experts" as the top-cited option in the dataset, but for someone whose community has historically been misrepresented by academic institutions and government bodies, placing institutional credibility at the top of a ranked list is alienating rather than resonant. The question "who do you trust most for reliable information" offers family and friends as an option but does not weight community elders, oral knowledge traditions, or peer networks built through professional Discord servers — sources Wiremu actually uses.

Wiremu's current workarounds are imperfect. He has developed a personal rule of closing all non-work browser tabs after 9 PM and uses a separate device for personal browsing to create a psychological boundary that his physical environment — a studio apartment with one desk — cannot provide. He follows Indigenous tech commentators on Mastodon rather than mainstream social media because he finds the content less triggering and more honest about AI's labour implications. When he encounters information he cannot verify, he tends to ignore it rather than engage, consistent with the eight percent of respondents in the dataset who report "Ignore" as their first verification step.

Designing for Wiremu would force the product to interrogate a core assumption embedded in nearly every survey item: that digital time is a choice. His profile would reveal that the system's wellbeing framing is calibrated for people who have the option to log off, and that building for those without that option requires entirely new question logic around compelled versus voluntary usage. It would also surface the cultural homogeneity of the trust and values architecture, pushing designers toward more pluralistic models of what "authentic content" and "reliable sources" actually mean across different knowledge traditions.

In a scenario of extreme use, Wiremu is midway through a twelve-hour contract sprint on a Tuesday when a client sends a link to an AI-generated market analysis document and asks him to verify its accuracy before presenting it to stakeholders. He has no institutional affiliation that would give him database access, no time to cross-reference academic sources, and no energy left for careful lateral reading. The survey's recommended first step — "look for multiple sources" — would require approximately forty minutes he does not have. He pastes the document into a competing AI tool, which confidently endorses the original's claims. He sends the analysis forward with a caveat that he cannot fully verify it. Later, lying awake, he answers a notification from this survey on his phone, and when it asks him to describe in one word how he feels after long periods online, he types "hollow" — a response that lands in the dataset's long tail of unique single-word responses, invisible to any aggregated analysis.

**Persona Two**

Edith Schwartz is 77 years old, retired, living alone in a mid-sized city in the American South after the death of her husband three years ago. She is among the oldest respondents in a dataset whose age distribution peaks in the 25–34 bracket, and she represents a category — the digitally active elderly widow in a low-income fixed-income household — that is both present in the data and nearly invisible to its analytical architecture. She spends between two and four hours online daily, primarily for what she describes as "necessary chores" such as banking, prescription refills, and staying in contact with her adult children who live out of state. She did not grow up with computers, learned to use a smartphone at sixty-eight with help from her daughter, and has no mental model of how algorithms, AI generation, or platform business models work.

Her extreme characteristics are a combination of very low digital literacy and high dependency in a context of social isolation. The dataset contains a small cohort of older respondents — those aged 55 and above represent roughly eighteen percent of the sample — and within that group, a subset report feeling "not at all confident" in distinguishing human from AI-generated content. Edith belongs to this group. Unlike younger low-confidence respondents who may choose not to trust as a protective default, Edith's low confidence is coupled with a pattern of provisional trust: she trusts what she is shown, particularly if it arrives via a familiar platform or mentions a recognisable institution. This combination — high trust in interface, low ability to interrogate content — makes her maximally vulnerable to manipulation while also making her one of the users who would benefit most from well-designed transparency features that the current landscape almost entirely lacks.

Her unmet needs include plain-language disclosure mechanisms for AI-generated content that do not require her to already know what AI is or how it works; a survey design that does not assume she has a vocabulary for emotional digital states beyond "tired" and "fine"; interfaces that account for motor impairment and reduced contrast sensitivity in ways that most platforms, including survey tools, do not; and a model of trust-building that acknowledges the specific exploitation vectors she faces — romance scams, health misinformation, and grandparent impersonation schemes — rather than treating trust as a general epistemological stance toward content. When the survey asks whether she trusts "big tech companies to use AI responsibly," the question presupposes she knows what big tech companies are and has a formed opinion about AI's responsible use. She does not. She trusts the interface she is using in that moment, which is a fundamentally different relationship with digital authority.

The barriers Edith faces are both cognitive and physical. The survey's trust-verification question asks what she does when unsure if content is real, and her honest answer — "I ask my daughter" — maps loosely onto the "ask friends or family" option, which only three percent of respondents in the dataset selected. The survey treats family consultation as a marginal coping strategy rather than the primary epistemological resource it is for digitally peripheral older users. A second barrier is the emotional vocabulary the survey demands: it asks for one-word descriptions of complex feeling states after online sessions, a form of introspective metacognition that requires both emotional granularity and a willingness to project inward that may be culturally or generationally unfamiliar. Edith tends to say she feels "fine" online, which the survey cannot distinguish from genuine neutral affect versus an absence of self-monitoring vocabulary.

Her current workaround is her daughter, who functions as a human content moderator, verification service, and technical support line rolled into one. When Edith receives an email that seems suspicious, she screenshots it and sends it via text. When she cannot figure out a form, she waits until Sunday when her daughter calls. When she sees something upsetting on the news app her daughter installed, she turns the phone screen down and makes tea. She has never reported encountering AI-generated content online because she does not know what that category is — and yet she has certainly encountered it, particularly in the health supplement advertisements that target her demographic with synthetic testimonials.

Designing for Edith would expose how thoroughly the product's entire epistemological framework assumes a user who has already internalised digital categories — content, platform, algorithm, AI — as natural kinds. Her persona would force a redesign of the trust and verification architecture from first principles, replacing assumed digital literacy with explicit interface-level transparency. It would also reveal that the system's notion of "coping with digital overwhelm" is built for users with abundant social infrastructure and time flexibility, rather than for isolated elderly people whose digital exhaustion is entangled with loneliness and dependency in ways the current model cannot parse.

Edith is sitting at her kitchen table on a Wednesday morning when an advertisement appears on her tablet for a medication that claims to have helped "thousands of women over 75 with joint pain." The testimonial video shows a woman who looks remarkably like someone from her church. She watches it twice, feels hopeful, and clicks the link. The checkout page asks for her Medicare number alongside her credit card. She pauses, uncertain. The survey she completed the previous week asked her how she verifies online information; she said she Googles it, but she does not do that now — she is tired, her hands hurt, and the page looks professional. She closes the tab, which the system interprets as a bounce and records as a coping mechanism. Nothing in the survey she completed would have captured either the near-miss or the reason she almost wasn't one.
