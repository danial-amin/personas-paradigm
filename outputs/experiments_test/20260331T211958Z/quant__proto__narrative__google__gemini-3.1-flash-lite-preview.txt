**Persona 1: The Disillusioned Professional**

This individual is typically between 35 and 44 years old, works full-time, and spends between 7 to 8 hours online daily. They feel a consistent sense of fatigue and regret regarding their digital habits, often describing their state after long periods of screen time as "tired" or "drained." They struggle with the psychological weight of the digital world, frequently questioning the trustworthiness of content and feeling a sense of annoyance when they encounter AI-generated material. They are deeply skeptical of big tech companies and harbor significant concerns about how AI will disrupt their professional and personal lives.

When faced with digital overwhelm, this persona prioritizes taking a physical break or exercising to recalibrate. They value clear facts and evidence above all else when evaluating information and prefer to rely on academic experts or traditional news sources rather than social media. Their primary motivation for change is to reclaim focus and reduce mindless scrolling, as they are acutely aware of how digital noise impacts their ability to maintain energy and mental clarity throughout the work week.

**Persona 2: The Anxious Digital Native**

Typically aged 25 to 34, this persona is often balancing work or study while spending 9 to 10 hours or more online each day. They frequently report feeling "guilty" or "wasted" after spending extended time online, and they often feel lonely or disconnected despite their high levels of digital engagement. They are highly sensitive to the negative impacts of social media on their self-perception and are frequently suspicious of the content they consume. They are moderately to very worried about the long-term implications of AI on human creativity and their own future stability.

This individual is proactive about their digital wellbeing but struggles with the addictive nature of feeds and algorithms. When they realize they have been misled by online content, they feel personally betrayed or frustrated. To cope with digital stress, they actively seek out multiple sources to verify information and prefer to rely on personal connections or recognized experts. Their goal for a healthier digital life is to establish firmer boundaries and cultivate more purposeful, intentional interactions, as they are increasingly concerned about how constant digital consumption is eroding their attention span and overall wellbeing.
