The first persona is Maya, a 34-year-old woman in the United States who works full-time and spends about 7–10 hours online each day. She uses digital spaces for work, staying informed, and keeping up with people, but she often ends the day feeling tired, drained, and a little guilty about time lost to scrolling. When her online time feels purposeful, especially for learning, family connection, or getting something done, she feels more focused and energized; when it doesn’t, she feels disconnected and self-conscious. She is highly skeptical of social content and frequently questions whether what she sees is real, especially on social media, images, and videos. If something seems false, she feels annoyed or disappointed and immediately looks for multiple sources or checks Google before believing it.

Maya trusts facts, clear evidence, and experts more than influencers or platform-driven content. She is strongly bothered by AI-generated content that is designed to manipulate attention, and she does not trust big tech to use AI responsibly. She is moderately confident she can spot AI content, but the uncertainty still bothers her because she worries about misinformation, authenticity, and the value of human creativity. When overwhelmed, her instinct is to take a break, step away from the phone, or exercise. A digital life coaching service would appeal to her if it helped her reduce passive scrolling, build healthier boundaries, and filter information more confidently without making her feel cut off from useful digital tools.

The second persona is Daniel, a 42-year-old man in the United Kingdom who works full-time and spends roughly 9–12 hours online each day, often because of work, news, and practical tasks. He sees digital technology as useful and efficient, especially for getting information quickly, managing work, and staying connected, but he also feels the strain: tiredness, distraction, and a sense that online life can become mindless or overwhelming. He is more likely than Maya to feel that online activity can be productive when it aligns with his values or work goals, yet he still often regrets how much time he spends online and wishes he could switch off more easily. He tends to feel annoyed or frustrated when content turns out to be misleading, and he is particularly wary of social media and news feeds.

Daniel’s trust style is pragmatic and evidence-led. He usually checks multiple sources, relies on academics or experts, and wants content backed by clear facts rather than hype. He is concerned about AI’s impact on his life, especially around misinformation, distraction, and the long-term effects on focus and mental energy, though he also recognizes that AI can improve productivity and convenience. When overloaded, he tends to step away, turn off his phone, or exercise, and he wants more control over how digital tools fit into his day. A digital life coaching service would resonate with him if it focused on reducing digital overload, improving focus and discipline, and helping him use technology intentionally without feeling constantly pulled by feeds, notifications, and low-value content.
