**Persona 1: Rachel Caldwell**

Rachel Caldwell is a 34-year-old woman working full-time in the United States. She is a mid-career professional who spends 7–8 hours online each day, much of it for work, and finds herself scrolling social media during breaks and before bed. Rachel's end goals when using a digital life coaching service are to reduce the time she spends passively scrolling social media so she can be more present with her family, and to develop a structured daily routine that includes intentional screen-free periods for rest and exercise. Her broader life goals are to feel like a competent, balanced mother and professional who models healthy habits rather than digital dependency, and to rebuild her capacity for sustained focus, which she feels has eroded over the past several years. During any interaction with such a service, Rachel wants to feel understood rather than judged for her habits, and to leave each session with a clear, manageable action she can take that day. Rachel frequently describes herself as "drained" or "tired" after extended time online, and she often regrets time spent on social media when she could have been with family or completing household tasks. She trusts academics and evidence-based sources more than any other authority and habitually cross-references information across multiple sources before accepting it. When overwhelmed by digital life, her instinct is to take a break, though she acknowledges she often returns to scrolling within minutes. On a typical weeknight, Rachel puts her children to bed and picks up her phone intending to check one notification. Forty-five minutes later she is deep in an Instagram feed of parenting content and home renovation videos, feeling simultaneously entertained and guilty. She opens the coaching app, which prompts her to reflect on what she actually wanted from the last half-hour and suggests a five-minute wind-down breathing exercise followed by reading a physical book. Rachel completes the exercise, sets a bedtime phone alarm for the next evening, and goes to sleep feeling more in control of her choices.

**Persona 2: James Whitmore**

James Whitmore is a 42-year-old man employed full-time in the United Kingdom in a knowledge-work role that requires him to be online 9–10 hours a day. He is concerned about misinformation, AI-generated content, and the declining quality of what appears in his news and social feeds. James's end goals are to develop a reliable personal system for quickly verifying whether online content is trustworthy, and to spend his limited non-work screen time on learning and purposeful activities rather than doom-scrolling news and social media. His life goals center on becoming a well-informed citizen who can confidently discuss current events without feeling manipulated, and on protecting his children from the same trust erosion he experiences. He wants to feel empowered and sharp during any coaching interaction, not patronized or overwhelmed by more information. James questions online content often, sometimes almost always, and he distrusts social media and AI-generated images the most. He looks for multiple sources when uncertain and gravitates toward academic experts for reliable information. He is moderately confident in detecting AI content but deeply bothered when he discovers he has engaged with something fabricated, describing the feeling as "cheated" or "deceived." When digital life feels too much, he exercises—usually a run—to reset. One evening James encounters a viral news story that provokes outrage. Rather than sharing it immediately, he opens the coaching platform, which guides him through a three-step verification checklist: check the original source, look for corroboration from an established institution, and pause for twenty-four hours before acting on emotional content. The tool also surfaces a short article from a trusted academic on media literacy. James completes the checklist, discovers the story was misleadingly framed, and feels a mix of relief and frustration—but also a growing confidence in his own discernment.

**Persona 3: David Osei**

David Osei is a 27-year-old man who is currently unemployed and living in Canada. He spends 9–10 hours online daily, partly looking for work and partly consuming entertainment and social media to cope with boredom and low mood. David's end goals are to redirect at least two hours of his daily online time toward skill-building or job-search activities that move him closer to employment, and to reduce the shame and regret he feels after long, unstructured scrolling sessions. His life goals are to find stable work that gives him a sense of purpose and financial independence, and to feel less isolated and more socially connected in the real world rather than through a screen. During interactions with a coaching service, David wants to feel encouraged rather than lectured, and to sense that small steps genuinely count. David describes his post-scrolling state as "empty" or "numb," and he often feels lonely or disconnected after spending time online, even though he came online seeking connection. He frequently regrets time spent on social media but finds it difficult to stop because the alternative feels like confronting the anxiety of his job search. He is wary of AI-generated content, frequently questions what he reads, and feels "annoyed" or "betrayed" when he discovers something was fake. He trusts no single authority in particular and relies on cross-checking sources himself. His go-to coping mechanism when overwhelmed is to take a break—usually stepping away from the screen entirely—but he often returns within the hour. On a Tuesday afternoon, David has been scrolling Reddit and watching YouTube videos for three hours after a discouraging round of job applications. He opens the coaching app, which acknowledges the difficulty of job searching and asks him to pick one twenty-minute task aligned with his values—updating a single section of his résumé or messaging a friend to meet for coffee. David chooses to message a friend. The app logs this as a meaningful action, offers a brief reflection prompt, and schedules a check-in for the following morning. David feels slightly lighter and texts his friend, who responds immediately with a plan to meet that weekend.

**Persona 4: Susan Hargrove**

Susan Hargrove is a 52-year-old woman working full-time in the United States who also provides informal caregiving for an aging parent. She spends 5–6 hours online each day, split between work tasks, health research, news consumption, and occasional social media. Susan's end goals are to stop doom-scrolling news feeds that leave her feeling anxious and drained, and to identify two or three trustworthy digital sources she can rely on for health and current-events information without second-guessing everything she reads. Her life goal is to protect her mental and physical energy so she can sustain the dual demands of career and caregiving without burning out. She wants to feel calm and in control during coaching interactions, as though someone competent is helping her simplify an overwhelming landscape. Susan often describes her energy after extended online time as "neutral" or "tired," and she sometimes feels lonely after social media use, though she rarely admits it. She questions online content frequently, especially news, and is only slightly confident in her ability to distinguish AI-generated material from human-created content. This uncertainty bothers her moderately; she worries about AI's societal effects but feels personally powerless to change them. She trusts traditional news outlets and academic experts, evaluates content primarily on whether it is supported by clear evidence, and copes with digital overwhelm by taking a break—often a walk or a cup of tea away from screens. On a Sunday evening, Susan has spent ninety minutes reading conflicting health articles about her parent's condition and is now anxious and unsure what to believe. She opens the coaching service, which guides her through a curated list of vetted medical resources and a brief journaling prompt: "What is one fact I know for certain, and one question I want to ask the doctor?" Susan writes down both, feels her anxiety decrease, and sets the phone on the charger in the kitchen rather than bringing it to bed.

**Persona 5: Liam Chen**

Liam Chen is a 20-year-old male university student in the United Kingdom who spends 7–8 hours online each day between coursework, social media, gaming, and short-form video platforms. He is acutely aware that his attention span has shortened and that comparison with peers on social media affects his self-image. Liam's end goals are to limit his consumption of short-form video content like TikTok and Instagram Reels to under thirty minutes per day, and to use AI tools for studying more effectively rather than as a replacement for his own thinking. His life goal is to graduate feeling genuinely knowledgeable and capable, not like someone who outsourced his education to technology. He wants interactions with a coaching service to feel quick, honest, and non-preachy—more like a trusted friend checking in than a lecture. Liam describes himself as "tired" and sometimes "ugly" or "insecure" after time on social media, and he occasionally feels lonely even though he is digitally connected to hundreds of people. He often questions whether online content is real, is moderately confident in spotting AI-generated material, and feels strongly bothered by the idea of AI content designed to manipulate his engagement. He worries quite a lot that AI will devalue human creativity—a concern that is personally relevant because he writes music. When overwhelmed, he tends to either take a break or turn off his phone entirely, though the pull of notifications makes this hard to sustain. Between lectures on a Wednesday, Liam opens TikTok for what he intends to be a two-minute break and surfaces forty minutes later, late for a seminar and annoyed with himself. That night he opens the coaching app, which shows him his self-reported screen-time trend for the week and asks him to choose one friction strategy: moving the TikTok icon off his home screen, setting a fifteen-minute app timer, or scheduling a specific short-form video window after his last class. Liam chooses the timer, sets it, and the app sends him an encouraging notification the next afternoon when he successfully closes the app at the limit.
